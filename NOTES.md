# Notes

Informal observations and context about the learner. These inform lesson design but don't belong in the formal learning record.

---

## Learner Profile

- **Background**: Adult male, native English speaker
- **Language history**: No prior exposure to any South Asian language (no Hindi, Urdu, or Bengali)
- **Motivation**: Family connection — wants to communicate directly with Bangladeshi in-laws rather than relying on his partner to translate
- **Learning style preference**: Strongly audio/verbal oriented; text-heavy or script-heavy materials are demotivating

## Family Context

- **Partner's family background**: Bangladeshi, based in Dhaka
- **Household**: Likely Muslim — Islamic greetings (Assalamu alaikum / Wa alaikum assalam) are the norm, not secular alternatives
- **Dialect target**: Dhaka spoken Bangla — this differs from West Bengal / Kolkata Bangla in vocabulary, accent, and some cultural registers
- **Exposure pattern**: Family visits and phone/video calls, not daily immersion. Sessions are relatively infrequent but high-stakes (learner wants to make a good impression)

## Key Cultural Notes to Weave Into Lessons

- Respect hierarchy is important in Bangladeshi family culture — using the right titles and the formal **apni** form with elders is not optional; it signals respect or its absence clearly
- Hospitality in Bangladeshi households is intense — the learner *will* be offered food and drink multiple times; knowing how to respond graciously in Bangla will be warmly received
- A man learning the language to connect with his wife's family will be seen as an act of respect and warmth — even imperfect Bangla will be appreciated
- Islamic greetings are the opener for *everything* in a Muslim household; getting Assalamu alaikum and its response right should be the very first thing learned

## What Lessons Should Avoid

- **Bengali script**: Never, under any circumstances. This learner is verbal-only.
- **Kolkata dialect features**: Don't teach "tumi" as the default "you" — Dhaka speakers will use it, but elders expect "apni"
- **Literary/formal Bangla (shadhubhasha)**: Not useful for this goal
- **Grammar-heavy explanations**: Patterns and examples yes; terminology and paradigm tables no
- **Long sessions**: 30–45 min max. Lessons should feel achievable, not overwhelming.

## What Makes a Good Session for This Learner

- Immediate practical value: "you will use this at the next family visit"
- Audio-first framing: phrases described by how they *sound*, not how they're spelled
- Cultural context: *why* something is said, not just *what* it means
- Real scenario practice: simulate arrival at the house, sitting down for tea, etc.
- Positive reinforcement: even small wins matter for adult learners starting from zero
