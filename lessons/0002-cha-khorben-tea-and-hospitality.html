The Scenario

What happens in the first 10 minutes

You're shown to the sitting room. Bosun — sit down. Before you've settled your coat, someone appears in the doorway: "Cha khaben?" Will you have tea?

This is not really a question. This is Bangladeshi hospitality in three syllables. The right answer is yes. And then the food appears. And then more food. This lesson gives you the language to navigate the next 30 minutes with warmth.
Core Phrases · The Hospitality Exchange

What they'll say — and what to say back

Learn these in order. They map to exactly what happens around the table.

Cha khaben?
chaa KHAA-ben
"Will you have tea?" — the first hospitality offer
You will hear this within 2 minutes of arriving. Every single time.
With friends (tumi) Cha khabe? / Ki khabe? — chaa KHAA-be — with a peer or friend the -ben ending (apni/formal) shifts to -be (tumi/familiar). Same warmth, different register.
Jee, dhônnobad
jee, DHÔN-no-baad
"Yes, thank you" — accept the tea
Always accept at first. Even if you don't drink tea, accepting the offer is the social act.
Also heard: "Shukriya" (SHUK-ri-yaa) — an Arabic/Urdu-influenced form that's widely used. Both are understood, but "dhônnobad" is the Shudho Bangla word.
Khub shundor hoyeche
khub SHUN-dor HO-ye-che
"It's come out beautifully" — complimenting food or tea
Use this after a first sip or bite. It's a genuine compliment that hosts love to hear.
Literally "very beautiful it has become" — Bangla compliments food with "beautiful" the way English uses "delicious". Both work.
Bhalo laglo
BHAA-lo LAAG-lo
"I liked it / that was lovely"
Versatile end-of-meal or end-of-visit phrase. Works for food, a conversation, the whole visit.
Aro neen
AA-ro neen
"Take more / have some more" — what the host says
You're not asking for this. They're insisting on it. Warmly. Repeatedly.
This is a command that's actually an act of love. Your hosts will say this many times. Knowing what it means lets you respond gracefully rather than looking confused.
Ektu nebo
EK-tu NE-bo
"I'll take a little" — the graceful response to "aro neen"
Better than refusing outright. "Ektu" (a little) is your friend — it accepts the hospitality while managing the quantity.
Ami bhoraa
AA-mi BHO-raa
"I'm full" — when you genuinely can't eat more
Deploy this with a smile and a hand gesture. Even so, "aro neen" may continue. That's fine — "bhoraa" is understood.
Khub mishti
khub MISH-ti
"Very sweet" — for desserts, mishti doi, gulab jamun
Bangladeshi sweets are very sweet. This is always true and always a compliment.
Moja holo!
MO-jaa HO-lo
"That was great fun / I enjoyed that!"
A warm, colloquial exclamation after something enjoyable — a meal, a funny story, a game with the kids.
"Moja" is warm and expressive — a natural exclamation in Shudho Bangla after something genuinely enjoyable.
Listening Practice · What You'll Hear

The real conversation

Recognising what they're saying is half the battle. Here's the hospitality loop, start to finish.

Them
Bosun, bosun.
BO-shun · "Sit, sit." — with a gesture to the sofa
You
Jee, dhônnobad.
"Yes, thank you." — as you sit
Them
Ki khaben? Cha neben?
ki KHAA-ben, chaa NE-ben · "What will you have? Will you take tea?"
You
Jee, cha khabo. Dhônnobad.
"Yes, I'll have tea. Thank you."
If you don't want tea: "Na, dhônnobad — jol deben?" (No thank you — could you give water?) is polite and understood.
Them
Khaan, khaan. Aro neen.
KHAAN KHAAN, AA-ro neen · "Eat, eat. Take more." — during the meal
You
Jee, ektu nebo. Khub shundor hoyeche.
"Yes, I'll take a little. It's come out beautifully."
Them
Bhaat kheyechen?
bhaat KHE-ye-chen · "Have you eaten (rice)?" — checking if you've eaten enough
You
Jee, kheyechi. Khub bhalo hoyeche.
"Yes, I've eaten. It was very good."
Or if you haven't eaten yet: "Na, ekhono khaina" (No, I haven't eaten yet) — this will be fixed immediately.
With friends (tumi) Bhaat kheyecho? — bhaat KHE-ye-cho — a friend asking the same question. The -chen ending (apni) becomes -cho (tumi).
Them
Aro ektu mishti neben?
AA-ro EK-tu MISH-ti NE-ben · "Will you have a little more sweet?"
You
Ami bhoraa. Khub mishti chilo!
"I'm full. It was very sweet (and delicious)!"

Bonus · Small Talk at the Table

Three phrases for conversation

Once you're settled with tea and food, conversation flows. These three are your starting points.

Kaaj kaamon cholche?
kaaj KAA-mon CHOL-che
"How's work going?" — asking about someone's work
Use with your brother-in-law, your father-in-law. Shows you're interested in their life.
Bacchara kaamon ache?
bach-CHA-ra KAA-mon AA-che
"How are the children?" — asking about kids in the family
A warm opener, especially with parents or grandparents. Bacchara = the children (plural).
Shoreer kaamon?
SHO-reer KAA-mon
"How is your health?" — asking after an elder's wellbeing
Particularly good for grandparents. Shows care. Expect a detailed answer.
Asking about health is a sign of deep respect for elders in Bangladeshi families — never skip this with grandparents.
Real-World Challenge
Before your next visit

Next time you're offered tea or food — anywhere, even at work — mentally rehearse the hospitality loop: Jee, dhônnobad → Ektu nebo → Khub shundor hoyeche → Bhalo laglo.

The goal: when Amma says "Aro neen" at the next visit, you respond with "Ami bhoraa — khub shundor hoyeche" without thinking. That moment will mean a lot.

Bonus: try saying "Moja holo!" after something fun at the table. Watch the reaction.
