Meeting Your In-Laws

Everything you need for the first five minutes at the door — greetings, titles, and the phrases that show you've made an effort.

The scenario: You arrive at your in-laws' home. The door opens. In the next 5 minutes you'll greet everyone, be invited in, be asked to sit, and almost certainly be offered tea. This lesson gives you the exact phrases for each moment.
Section 1

Core Phrases

Learn these eight phrases and you're ready for the door. Read each one, say it aloud, and notice the pronunciation notes.

Assalamu alaikum
as-SAA-laa-mu aa-LAY-kum
"Peace be upon you" — the Islamic greeting
This is how you greet everyone. Use it when you arrive, when you enter a room, when you get on a call. In a Muslim household it is the opener for everything — more than "hello", it's a blessing. Say it to the whole room, not just one person.
They will respond:
Wa alaikum assalam
waa aa-LAY-kum as-SAA-lam — "And upon you peace"
Dhaka note In everyday Dhaka speech, this is sometimes shortened slightly in casual exchanges, but in a family greeting context — especially with elders — say the full form clearly. It signals sincerity.
Kemon achen?
KE-mon AA-chen
"How are you?" (respectful)
Use this with your in-laws and any elders. The word achen is the respectful form of "are" — it tells the listener you're treating them with respect. This is the question to ask right after the greeting.
They may respond:
Bhalo achi, apni kemon achen?
BHAA-lo AA-chi, AP-ni KE-mon AA-chen — "I'm well, how are you?" (and they'll ask back)
Note In Shudho Bangla, achen is the standard respectful form — use it consistently with elders. In very casual speech you may occasionally hear it shortened, but achen is always correct and appropriate.
With friends (tumi) Kemon acho? — KE-mon AA-cho — use this with your wife's friends and peers of similar age. The verb ending shifts from -en (apni/formal) to -o (tumi/familiar).
Jee, bhalo achi
jee, BHAA-lo AA-chi
"Yes, I'm well"
Your response when someone asks how you are. Always lead with jee — it's a polite acknowledgement that signals respect. Then bhalo achi = I'm well. Simple, clean, correct.
Note Starting answers with jee is a marker of respect in Shudho Bangla, especially towards elders. Skipping it and going straight to bhalo achi isn't rude, but jee, bhalo achi sounds more natural and warmer in a family setting.
Ashun, ashun
AA-shun, AA-shun
"Come in, come in"
You will hear this the moment the door opens — a warm, almost reflexive invitation to enter. Saying it yourself if you ever answer the door at their home would be a lovely touch.
With friends (tumi) Ashho — AA-sho — the command form softens with the tumi register. Ashun (apni) becomes ashho (tumi).
Bosun
BO-shun
"Please sit down"
You'll hear this immediately after being welcomed inside. In return, if you're ever playing host, using bosun yourself will surprise and delight them.
Note Literally "sit" in the respectful command form. You may also hear it repeated warmly — bosun, bosun — as a sign of hospitality and welcome.
With friends (tumi) Bosho — BO-sho — same meaning, familiar command form. Bosun (apni) → bosho (tumi).
Dhônnobad
DHÔN-no-baad
"Thank you"
The standard "thank you" in Shudho Bangla. Use it freely — when offered food, when someone fetches something, when you leave. This is the form your wife's family will feel most at home with.
Also heard:
Shukriya
SHUK-ri-yaa — borrowed from Arabic via Urdu, also common in Bangladeshi Muslim households. Both are understood; dhônnobad is the Bangla-native form.
Amma / Abba
AM-maa / AB-baa
Mother-in-law / Father-in-law (how you address them directly)
These are the titles you use when speaking directly to your in-laws. Not their name — their title. Using these correctly signals that you understand and respect Bangladeshi family structure. It will mean a lot.
Note Some families use Ma instead of Amma, and Baba instead of Abba. Ask your partner which their family uses. Either is warm and correct.
Khoda hafez
KHO-daa HAA-fez
"Goodbye" — lit. "God protect you"
The standard farewell in a Muslim Bangladeshi household. Use this when leaving rather than just "bye". Heard at the end of every visit and call.
Also used:
Allah hafez
AL-laa HAA-fez — more explicitly Islamic, increasingly common. Both are correct and warm.
Section 2

What You'll Hear

Recognising what they say is half the battle. These are the phrases that will come at you in the first five minutes. Know what they mean so you can respond naturally — even a nod and jee shows you understood.

Ashun, ashun — bôsun
AA-shun AA-shun — BO-shun
"Come in, come in — please sit"
Dhônnobad, Amma / Dhônnobad, Abba
Kemon achen? / Kemon acho?
KE-mon AA-chen / KE-mon AA-cho
"How are you?" — the first may be directed at you respectfully; the second is casual between peers
Jee, bhalo achi — apni kemon achen?
Ki khaben? Cha neben?
ki KHAA-ben — chaa NE-ben
"What will you have? Will you have tea?" — you will hear this within 90 seconds of arriving
Jee, dhônnobad — or — Jee, cha nebo (yes, I'll have tea)
Bhaat kheyechen?
bhaat KHE-ye-chen
"Have you eaten?" — Bangla hospitality, asking if you've had rice (the staple). Not literally asking about rice — it means "have you eaten a proper meal?"
Jee, kheyechi (yes, I've eaten) — or — Na, khainai (no, not yet)
Thik ache? / Thik acho?
THEEK aa-che / THEEK aa-cho
"Are you alright?" — checking in on you. Often said if you look tired or quiet.
Jee, thik achi — "Yes, I'm fine"
Abar ashben
AA-bar AAS-ben
"Come again" — said as you leave. A warm farewell. Not a question — it's an invitation.
Jee, ashbo — "Yes, I'll come"
With friends (tumi) Abar ashbe — AA-bar AAS-be — a peer or friend would say this. The -ben ending (apni) shifts to -be (tumi).

Before the next lesson

Real-World Challenge

Before your next session: ask your partner to play the role of their mother or father. You knock, they open the door. Run through the arrival — greeting, being invited in, sitting down, being offered tea, and saying goodbye.

Focus on just three phrases until they feel automatic: Assalamu alaikum, Kemon achen?, and Jee, bhalo achi. These are the ones that matter most in the first 30 seconds.

If that feels like too much — just practise saying Assalamu alaikum naturally, ten times, until it doesn't feel strange in your mouth.
