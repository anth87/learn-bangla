# Resources: Spoken Bangla (Shudho Bangla)

> This list is curated for a verbal-only learner targeting Shudho Bangla (standard Bangla), as spoken by a Bangladeshi family. Resources focused on reading/writing Bengali script are excluded unless they have strong audio components.

---

## Knowledge
> Structured input — courses, audio programs, and reference material.

### Pimsleur Bengali
**Link**: https://www.pimsleur.com/learn-bengali
**Format**: Audio (30-min lessons)
**Cost**: Paid (subscription or one-time purchase)
**Reach for this when**: You want the single best audio-first foundation — this is your primary resource for building spoken patterns from scratch.

Pimsleur's spaced-repetition audio method is ideal for this mission: entirely spoken, no reading required, structured so you speak from lesson one. The Bengali course covers standard Dhaka-leaning Bangla (not Kolkata dialect). Lessons are exactly 30 minutes — perfect for the learner's session constraint. Do these first and consistently before adding other resources.

---

### BanglaPod101 (Innovative Language)
**Link**: https://www.bengali.pod101.com
**Format**: Audio + video lessons with transcripts
**Cost**: Freemium (basic free, premium subscription for full access)
**Reach for this when**: You want structured lessons with a native speaker voice and cultural notes, especially for conversational phrases.

Organised by level (Absolute Beginner through Advanced). The audio dialogues with line-by-line breakdown are excellent for picking apart what you hear in real conversation. Skip the script-reading elements — focus on the audio and the romanised pronunciation guides. Cultural notes are particularly useful for household and family contexts.

---

### Easy Bangla (YouTube)
**Link**: https://www.youtube.com/@EasyBangla (search "Easy Bangla" on YouTube)
**Format**: Video lessons
**Cost**: Free
**Reach for this when**: You want short, digestible video explanations of Bangla phrases and pronunciation from a native speaker.

A YouTube channel dedicated to making Bangla accessible to beginners. Lessons are in English with clear pronunciation modelling. Good supplement to Pimsleur — use it to hear how phrases actually sound from a native Dhaka speaker, and to cross-check your pronunciation intuitions.

---

### Forvo — Bengali Pronunciations
**Link**: https://forvo.com/languages/bn/
**Format**: Audio (single words and phrases, native speaker recordings)
**Cost**: Free (with limits); premium removes limits
**Reach for this when**: You encounter a specific word in the glossary or a lesson and want to hear it pronounced by a real native speaker.

Forvo is a crowd-sourced pronunciation dictionary. Search any Bangla word (in romanisation or Bengali script) and hear multiple native speakers say it. Invaluable for double-checking pronunciation before a real family visit. Tip: when multiple recordings exist, prefer ones labelled from Bangladesh over India for Dhaka-accurate pronunciation.

---

### Teach Yourself Complete Bengali
**Link**: Available on Amazon and major booksellers
**Format**: Book + audio download
**Cost**: Paid (~£15–25)
**Reach for this when**: You want a structured reference that explains *why* things work the way they do — grammar patterns, sentence structure — without requiring you to read the script.

The audio component is strong and the course uses romanised Bangla alongside script. You can follow the audio track while ignoring the script sections entirely. Useful as a second-layer reference once Pimsleur lessons start raising questions about sentence structure.

---

### Colloquial Bengali (Routledge)
**Link**: https://routledgetextbooks.com/textbooks/colloquial/language/bengali.php
**Format**: Book + audio
**Cost**: Paid
**Reach for this when**: You want a more in-depth reference for grammar and colloquial patterns once you're past the beginner phase.

More detailed than Teach Yourself, with good coverage of colloquial spoken forms. Audio recordings are clear. Like Teach Yourself, you can use the audio independently. Better for intermediate use than as a starting point.

## Skills
> Practice tools — drills, active recall, conversation practice.

### Anki (Spaced Repetition Flashcards)
**Link**: https://apps.ankiweb.net
**Format**: App (iOS, Android, desktop)
**Cost**: Free (desktop + Android); iOS app is paid
**Reach for this when**: You want to drill vocabulary and phrases with proven spaced repetition — especially for memorising the glossary terms.

The most effective flashcard system available. Build decks using phonetic spellings only (no script). Put the English meaning on the front, the phonetic Bangla on the back — and crucially, practise *saying* the Bangla out loud before revealing. There are pre-made Bengali Anki decks available (search AnkiWeb) but custom decks built from this glossary will be more targeted.

---

### iTalki
**Link**: https://www.italki.com
**Format**: Video call lessons with native speakers
**Cost**: Paid per lesson (community tutors ~$10–20/hr; professional teachers more)
**Reach for this when**: You're ready to attempt real conversation and want a patient native Bangladeshi speaker to practise with.

iTalki lets you book 1-on-1 video lessons with native speakers. Filter for Bangladeshi tutors specifically. Even 30-minute "conversation sessions" with a community tutor (cheaper than professional teachers) are extremely valuable. Brief the tutor: "I only want spoken Bangla, no script, Shudho Bangla, household/family topics." Good to start once you have ~10–15 lessons of Pimsleur under your belt.

---

### HelloTalk / Tandem
**Link**: https://www.hellotalk.com / https://www.tandem.net
**Format**: App — text, voice, and video exchange with native speakers
**Cost**: Free (with premium options)
**Reach for this when**: You want informal practice with native Bangladeshi speakers between structured lessons.

Language exchange apps where you help someone with English in exchange for Bangla practice. Focus on voice messages rather than text (this keeps the learning verbal). Search for partners from Bangladesh. Less structured than iTalki but more natural and free.

## Wisdom
> Communities and real-world use — where the language actually lives.

### r/learn_bengali (Reddit)
**Link**: https://www.reddit.com/r/learn_bengali/
**Format**: Community forum
**Cost**: Free
**Reach for this when**: You have a specific question ("how do I address my wife's uncle?"), want to check a pronunciation, or want to hear from other learners and native speakers.

A small but helpful community. Bangladeshi native speakers do respond to questions. Good for cultural nuance questions that language apps won't answer — like exactly which title to use for a specific family relationship in a Dhaka Muslim household.

---

### YouTube: Bangladeshi family vlogs and TV
**Link**: Search YouTube for "Bangladesh family vlog" or "Bangladeshi cooking channel"
**Format**: Video
**Cost**: Free
**Reach for this when**: You want passive listening exposure to natural Dhaka household Bangla in context.

Watching Bangladeshi family YouTube channels — cooking, daily life, travel — gives you ear training in real informal Dhaka speech. You won't understand much at first, but over time you'll start catching familiar phrases. Even 10–15 minutes of background listening after a lesson session is valuable. The accent, rhythm, and vocabulary will begin to feel familiar.

---

### Your Partner and Family (Primary Wisdom Source)
**Format**: Real life
**Cost**: Free
**Reach for this when**: Always — this is the whole point.

Your partner is the best resource in the room. Ask them to say phrases for you, correct your pronunciation, and explain what family members said. Ask them to use a Bangla phrase or two with you regularly, even in English conversations. The family visits are practice sessions — arrive with one or two phrases you've drilled, use them, and notice the reaction.
