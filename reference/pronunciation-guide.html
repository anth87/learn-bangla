Pronunciation Guide

How Bangla sounds — using English word comparisons. No script, no symbols you need to learn separately.

This guide is verbal-only. We never use Bengali script. Instead, every sound is mapped to an English word you already know. Read the phonetics in your lessons using this page as your key.
Chapter 1

The Essential Vowels

Bangla has more vowel sounds than English. These are the ones you'll encounter constantly — get them right and you'll be understood.

aa Long A Very common
Like the 'a' in father or car — open, held slightly longer than in English.
aassen come in bhaaaat rice AM-maa mother BHAA-lo good/well
Think of it as making your mouth more open than for the English 'a' in 'cat'. Drop your jaw slightly.
ô Short O Very common
Like the 'o' in not or hot in British English — open and unrounded. Not the 'o' in 'go'.
BO-shun sit KHO-daa God bô-sun sit (variant) gô-rôm hot
This is NOT the 'o' in 'go' or 'bone'. It's short and open — like when a doctor asks you to say "ahh" and your mouth is open.
ee Long EE Common
Like 'ee' in see or feel — clear and held.
jee yes (polite) SHUK-ri-yaa thank you AA-chi I am
e Short E Common
Like 'e' in bed or get — short and clear.
KE-mon how AA-chen are (respectful)
u / oo OO sound Common
Like 'oo' in food — rounded and clear. Written 'u' in most lessons.
BUJ-hi understand TU-mi you (casual) SHUK-ri-yaa thank you
Chapter 2

Aspirated Consonants

Aspiration is the key feature of Bangla (and all South Asian languages) that English doesn't have. An aspirated consonant is the same sound as the base consonant, but with a puff of breath added. Hold your hand in front of your mouth — you should feel air.

Why this matters In English, pin vs spin — the 'p' in "pin" already has aspiration; "spin" doesn't. In Bangla, aspiration is a full distinction that changes meaning. But for a beginner, the main goal is: when you see kh / bh / dh / th / ph / gh / ch / jh, add a noticeable puff of breath.
kh
Like 'k' + breath
Similar to Scottish loch
gh
Like 'g' + breath
Breathy 'g' sound
ch
Like 'ch' in chair
Standard English ch
jh
Like 'j' + breath
Breathy 'j' sound
th
Like 't' + breath
NOT as in the — it's t + puff
dh
Like 'd' + breath
Breathy 'd' — as in dhow
bh
Like 'b' + breath
Soft breathy 'b' — bhalo
ph
Like 'p' + breath
NOT 'f' — it's p + puff
Important: th ≠ English "th" In Bangla, th is never like the 'th' in the or think. It's always a 't' sound with an added puff of air. So thik (meaning "correct/fine") is said "TEEK" with a breath — not like "thick".
Chapter 3

Retroflex Sounds

Retroflex consonants are made with the tongue curled back to touch the roof of the mouth. They're common in South Asian languages and absent in standard English. Don't panic — for casual speech, most listeners will understand you even if your retroflex isn't perfect.

ṭ / ḍ Retroflex T and D
Curl your tongue tip back so it points towards the roof of your mouth. Then make a 't' or 'd' sound. The result is fuller and more resonant than the standard 't' or 'd'.
Shortcut for beginners: In the phonetic spellings in these lessons, retroflex sounds are written in bold or noted where they appear. If in doubt, use a regular 't' or 'd' — native speakers will understand you. Over time, you'll hear the difference and your ear will guide your mouth.
tor-KA-ri vegetable dish ṭhik correct, fine
Chapter 4

Nasal Vowels

Some Bangla vowels are nasalised — air flows through the nose as well as the mouth. In lessons, we mark this with a tilde (~) after the vowel.

~ Nasalisation
Like the French vowels in bon or vin — or the 'n' in an American singer's pronunciation of "ain't". The vowel resonates in your nose.
In everyday speech, nasalisation is present but not always strongly marked. Don't over-nasal — a slight nasal quality is enough.
Chapter 5

Word Stress

Bangla stress patterns are fairly consistent — once you know the rule, you can apply it to new words automatically.

  • 1 Stress the first syllable in most Bangla words. KE-mon, BHAA-lo, BO-shun, AM-maa, AB-baa.
  • 2 Loanwords and Arabic/Urdu borrowings may keep their original stress. Assalamu alaikum: as-SAA-laa-mu aa-LAY-kum — the stress falls on the syllable that carries weight in Arabic.
  • 3 Two-syllable words almost always stress the first: BHAa-lo, THEek, CHAa (tea), DAal (lentils).
  • 4 Bangla does not use pitch to change meaning (it's not tonal like Chinese). Stress is about syllable weight, not a rising or falling melody.
Natural speech rhythm Even Shudho Bangla spoken naturally is slightly faster and more flowing than a slow lesson recording. Vowels may shorten a little in connected speech. Don't be surprised when family conversation sounds faster than what you've practised — it's the same words at a natural pace.

The most common colloquial shift: ch → s/sh If you ever hear "Kemon asen?" instead of "Kemon achen?", it's the same phrase — the 'ch' in verb endings sometimes softens to an 's' sound in relaxed speech. Your family's Shudho Bangla will generally use the full 'ch' form, but knowing this shift means you'll still recognise what you're hearing.
