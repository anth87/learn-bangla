# Glossary: Spoken Bangla (Shudho Bangla)

> Phonetic spellings only — no Bengali script. This glossary uses a simple English-friendly romanisation:
> - **aa** = long 'a' as in "father"
> - **ô** = the 'o' in "not" (very common in Bangla)
> - **kh, gh, bh, dh, th, ph** = aspirated consonants (the letter + a puff of air)
> - **~** after a vowel = slightly nasalised
> Stress is generally on the first syllable.

---

## Greetings & Courtesy

**Assalamu alaikum** *(as-SAA-laa-mu aa-LAY-kum)*: "Peace be upon you" — the standard Islamic greeting used in Muslim households. Use this to greet anyone; it's always appropriate.
  - _Response_: Wa alaikum assalam *(waa aa-LAY-kum as-SAA-lam)*

**Wa alaikum assalam** *(waa aa-LAY-kum as-SAA-lam)*: The response to Assalamu alaikum. Always respond with this — not responding is considered rude.

**Namaskar** *(naa-mas-KAR)*: Hindu/secular greeting equivalent to "hello". You may hear this from Hindu family members or in secular contexts.
  - _Avoid_: Don't use namaskar as a default in a Muslim household — use Assalamu alaikum instead.

**Khoda hafez** *(KHO-daa HAA-fez)*: "Goodbye" — lit. "God protect you". The standard farewell in Dhaka Muslim households.
  - _Also heard as_: Allah hafez *(AL-laa HAA-fez)* — more explicitly Islamic, increasingly common.
  - _Avoid_: "Bye bye" is understood but sounds very foreign; use khoda hafez with elders.

**Dhônnobad** *(DHÔN-no-baad)*: "Thank you" — the Bangla-native form. Standard in Shudho Bangla.
  - _Also heard as_: Shukriya *(SHUK-ri-yaa)* — borrowed from Arabic/Urdu, also common in Bangladeshi Muslim households. Both are understood.

**Maaf kôrben** *(MAAF kôr-BEN)*: "Please forgive me / excuse me / sorry". Polite apology or excuse-me.

**Apni ki bhalo achen?** *(AP-ni ki BHAA-lo AA-chen)*: "Are you well?" — respectful form (apni = formal 'you', used for elders).

**Kemon achen?** *(KE-mon AA-chen)*: "How are you?" — respectful/neutral form. Very common opener.
  - _Dhaka note_: In casual speech this often sounds like "Kemon asen?" — the 'ch' softens.

**Jee, bhalo achi** *(jee, BHAA-lo AA-chi)*: "Yes, I'm well" — polite response to "how are you?"
  - _Note_: Always lead with **jee** (yes/acknowledgement) when responding to elders — it signals respect.

**Jee** *(jee)*: "Yes" — the polite form. Use with elders and anyone you're showing respect to.
  - _Avoid_: "Haa" *(haa)* means yes too but is informal — use jee with elders.

**Na** *(naa)*: "No."

## Address & Family Titles

**Amma** *(AM-maa)*: Mother / mother-in-law. How you address your mother-in-law directly.
  - _Also heard as_: Ma *(maa)* — used in many families.

**Abba** *(AB-baa)*: Father / father-in-law. How you address your father-in-law directly.
  - _Also heard as_: Baba *(BAA-baa)*.

**Bhai** *(bhai — rhymes with 'pie')*: Brother. Also used broadly as "mate/friend" between men of similar age.

**Apa** *(AA-paa)*: Older sister. Also used respectfully for any older woman (e.g. a sister-in-law older than you).

**Bhaabi** *(BHAA-bi)*: Brother's wife / sister-in-law. How female in-laws of that relation are addressed.

**Shashuri** *(SHAA-shu-ri)*: Mother-in-law (formal term when talking *about* her, not to her face — say Amma to her directly).

**Shoshur** *(SHO-shur)*: Father-in-law (formal term when talking *about* him — say Abba to his face).

**Nana** *(NAA-naa)*: Maternal grandfather.

**Nani** *(NAA-ni)*: Maternal grandmother.

**Dada** *(DAA-daa)*: Paternal grandfather (in Dhaka usage).
  - _Note_: In West Bengal "dada" means older brother — different meaning.

**Dadi** *(DAA-di)*: Paternal grandmother.

**Chacha** *(CHAA-chaa)*: Father's younger brother (uncle).

**Chachi** *(CHAA-chi)*: Father's younger brother's wife (aunt).

**Mama** *(MAA-maa)*: Mother's brother (uncle).

**Mami** *(MAA-mi)*: Mother's brother's wife (aunt).

**Apni** *(AP-ni)*: "You" — formal/respectful. Always use this with elders and in-laws.
  - _Avoid_: Tumi *(TU-mi)* = informal "you" for peers. Tui *(tui)* = very intimate/casual. Don't use these with elders.

## Food & Hospitality (at the table)

**Cha khaben?** *(chaa KHAA-ben)*: "Will you have tea?" — the first hospitality offer you'll hear on every visit.
  - _Note_: "Khaben" is the Shudho Bangla form. In colloquial Dhaka speech you may also hear "khorben", but khaben is the standard form.

**Ektu** *(EK-tu)*: "A little." Use with nebo: "ektu nebo" = "I'll take a little" — the graceful response to being urged to eat more.

**Nebo** *(NE-bo)*: "I'll take / I'll have." First-person future of "neoa" (to take).

**Ami bhoraa** *(AA-mi BHO-raa)*: "I'm full." The polite signal you've had enough. May not stop "aro neen" — that's normal.

**Khub shundor hoyeche** *(khub SHUN-dor HO-ye-che)*: "It's come out beautifully" — the standard food/cooking compliment.
  - _Note_: Bangla compliments food with "beautiful" where English uses "delicious". Both shundor and mishti work.

**Khaan, khaan** *(KHAAN KHAAN)*: "Eat, eat!" — host encouragement during a meal. A command that is pure warmth.

**Kaaj kaamon cholche?** *(kaaj KAA-mon CHOL-che)*: "How's work going?" — small-talk opener for adults.

**Bacchara kaamon ache?** *(bach-CHA-ra KAA-mon AA-che)*: "How are the children?" — warm opener with parents or grandparents.

**Shoreer kaamon?** *(SHO-reer KAA-mon)*: "How is your health?" — respectful question for elders. Always ask this with grandparents.

**Moja holo!** *(MO-jaa HO-lo)*: "That was great / that was fun!" — warm, colloquial Dhaka expression of enjoyment.

## Everyday Conversation

**Ashun** *(AA-shun)*: "Come in / please come." What you'll hear at the door.

**Bosun** *(BO-shun)*: "Please sit down." Very common upon arrival.

**Ki khaben?** *(ki KHAA-ben)*: "What will you eat/drink?" — hospitality offer, you'll hear this immediately.

**Bhaat kheyechen?** *(bhaat KHE-ye-chen)*: "Have you eaten rice?" — a Bangla way of asking if you've eaten, rice being the staple.

**Khub bhalo** *(khub BHAA-lo)*: "Very good / very well." All-purpose positive response.

**Thik ache** *(THEEK aa-che)*: "It's fine / okay / that's alright." Very versatile.

**Bojheni** *(BOJ-he-ni)*: "I didn't understand."

**Aaste bolun** *(AAS-te BO-lun)*: "Please speak slowly."

**Ki bôllen?** *(ki BÔLL-en)*: "What did you say?" — polite way to ask for a repeat.

**Ami bujhi na** *(AA-mi BUJ-hi naa)*: "I don't understand."

**Ami Bangla kom jaani** *(AA-mi BANG-laa kom JAA-ni)*: "I know a little Bangla." — useful self-introduction.

**Bhalo laglo** *(BHAA-lo LAAG-lo)*: "I liked it / that was nice." Use after a meal, a visit, etc.

**Abar ashbo** *(AA-bar AAS-bo)*: "I'll come again." A warm farewell phrase.

## Food & Meals

**Bhaat** *(bhaat)*: Cooked rice. The staple. "Bhaat khawa" = eating (lit. eating rice).

**Dal** *(daal)*: Lentil soup/curry. A daily staple.

**Torkari** *(tôr-KA-ri)*: Vegetable dish / curry (general term).

**Mach** *(maach)*: Fish. Central to Bangladeshi cuisine.

**Mangsho** *(MANG-shô)*: Meat (usually goat/beef in Dhaka households).

**Roti** *(RO-ti)*: Flatbread.

**Jol** *(jol)*: Water — the standard Bangla/Shudho Bangla word.
  - _Also heard as_: Pani *(PAA-ni)* — used in parts of Bangladesh, borrowed from Hindi/Urdu. Both are understood.

**Cha** *(chaa)*: Tea. You will be offered this constantly.

**Mishti** *(MISH-ti)*: Sweet / sweets / dessert.

**Aro neen** *(AA-ro neen)*: "Take more / have more." What hosts say when serving food — a command that is really a warm invitation.

## Feelings & States

**Thaka** *(THAA-kaa)*: Tired. "Ami thaka" = I'm tired (informal).

**Bhalo lage** *(BHAA-lo LAA-ge)*: "I feel good / I like it." Lit. "feels good."

**Kharap lage** *(KHAA-raap LAA-ge)*: "I feel bad / I don't like it." Lit. "feels bad."

**Gôrôm** *(GÔ-rôm)*: Hot (weather or food).

**Thanda** *(THAN-daa)*: Cold.

**Bhook lagche** *(bhook LAAG-che)*: "I'm hungry." Lit. "hunger is feeling."

**Trishta lagche** *(TRISH-taa LAAG-che)*: "I'm thirsty."

**Shukhi** *(SHUK-hi)*: Happy.

**Moja** *(MO-jaa)*: Fun / enjoyment. "Moja holo!" = "That was fun!" Very colloquial, warm.
